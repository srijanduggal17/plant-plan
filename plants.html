Golden Pothos

Epipremnum aureum
🟢 Beginner-friendly · Very forgiving
💧
When to Water

Stick your finger 1–2 inches into the soil. If it feels dry at that depth, it's time to water. The top of the soil should look and feel dry, not just slightly pale. Pothos also tells you it's thirsty when its leaves start to look slightly droopy or dull — don't wait that long, but it's a good backup signal.

⚠️ Pothos is much more likely to die from overwatering than underwatering. When in doubt, wait another day.
🚿
How to Water

Water thoroughly — pour slowly around the base until water drains freely from the bottom. Then empty the saucer after 30 minutes so roots never sit in standing water. Use room-temperature water.

🗓
Expected Frequency

In your bright apartment, expect to water roughly:

Every 7–10 days in summer · Every 14 days in winter

Always check the soil — don't just go by the calendar.

☀️
Getting It to the Window
  • 1Good news: pothos is already fine in low to bright indirect light. South-facing windows can be intense though.
  • 2Start the plant 4–6 feet back from the window for the first 2 weeks.
  • 3Move it 1 foot closer every week. Watch for leaf bleaching or scorching (pale, washed-out patches) — if you see this, move it back a foot.
  • 4The ideal final spot is 2–3 feet from the glass, or right at the window if it's covered with a sheer curtain. Direct midday sun can scorch the leaves.

Fiddle Leaf Fig

Ficus lyrata
🔴 Intermediate · Needs consistency
💧
When to Water

Check the soil by pressing your finger 2 inches deep. Water only when it's dry at that depth. You can also lift the pot — a very light pot means dry soil. Look out for leaves that seem slightly less glossy or start to droop; that's a sign it's getting thirsty. Brown spots with yellow halos mean overwatering; dry, crispy brown edges mean underwatering.

🎯 Consistency is everything with FLFs. They hate change — same amount, same days, same spot.
🚿
How to Water

Water evenly and thoroughly around the entire pot until it drains from the bottom. Empty the saucer after 30 min. Use room-temp water — cold water can shock the roots. Avoid getting water on the leaves.

🗓
Expected Frequency

In a bright, warm apartment:

Every 7–10 days in growing season · Every 14–21 days in winter

Pick a day of the week and always check on that day — consistency reduces stress on the plant.

☀️
Getting It to the Window
  • 1Fiddle leaf figs love bright indirect light and will thrive near your south window — but acclimate slowly.
  • 2Place it 6–8 feet back from the window initially. Leave it there for 2 weeks without moving it at all.
  • 3Move it 1–2 feet closer every 2 weeks. Watch for leaf drop or brown spots — both signal stress.
  • 4Final ideal spot: 3–5 feet from the window with bright, indirect light most of the day. Some gentle morning sun is fine; harsh midday sun is not.
  • 5Do not rotate or move it once settled — FLFs drop leaves when disturbed. Pick a final spot and commit.

Snake Plant

Sansevieria trifasciata
🟢 Very easy · Nearly indestructible
💧
When to Water

Stick your finger all the way to your knuckle (2–3 inches) into the soil. Only water if it feels completely dry at that depth. You can also use a skewer — if it comes out clean and dry, it's time. Snake plants show overwatering as soft, mushy, yellowing leaves at the base. Underwatering shows as wrinkled or curling leaves.

💡 Snake plants can go weeks without water. The #1 killer is overwatering. Err heavily on the side of dry.
🚿
How to Water

Water deeply but infrequently — soak the soil fully until water comes out the drainage hole, then let it drain completely. Empty the saucer. Never let it sit in water. In winter, reduce watering to once a month or even less.

🗓
Expected Frequency

In a bright spot:

Every 2–4 weeks in summer · Every 4–8 weeks in winter

Always check with your finger first. In bright light it'll dry out faster.

☀️
Getting It to the Window
  • 1Snake plants tolerate almost any light, from deep shade to direct sun — they're one of the most adaptable plants out there.
  • 2Start 4–5 feet from the window for 1 week just to let it settle in from the nursery.
  • 3Move it 2 feet closer each week — you'll likely hit the window in 2–3 moves with no issues.
  • 4At the window: it can handle direct south-facing sun, though very harsh afternoon sun through glass may bleach leaves. A spot with morning to midday sun is ideal.
  • 5If leaves start looking pale or yellowish-white, move it back slightly. Otherwise, enjoy it right at the window!

White Bird of Paradise

Strelitzia nicolai
🟡 Moderate · Loves lots of light
💧
When to Water

Check by pushing your finger 2 inches into the soil. Water when the top 2 inches are dry but the deeper soil is still slightly cool. Unlike snake plants, birds of paradise don't want to go bone dry. Watch for drooping or curling leaves (underwatering) or yellowing lower leaves (overwatering).

🌿 In a bright south-facing window, this plant will grow fast and drink more. Check it weekly.
🚿
How to Water

Water thoroughly until it drains from the bottom, then empty the saucer. These plants like consistent moisture in summer — not soggy, but never fully dried out. In winter, let it dry a bit more between waterings. Use room-temperature water.

🗓
Expected Frequency

Near your bright window:

Every 7–10 days in summer · Every 14–21 days in winter

Adjust based on how quickly the soil dries — more light = faster drying = more frequent watering.

☀️
Getting It to the Window
  • 1Bird of paradise craves direct bright light — your south-facing window is actually the ideal spot. It will thrive there.
  • 2Start it 5–6 feet back from the window for the first 1–2 weeks. Nurseries usually keep plants in moderate light, so a slow intro prevents shock.
  • 3Move it 2 feet closer every week. Watch for crispy leaf edges (too intense too fast) or pale leaves (sunburn).
  • 4After 3–4 weeks, you can place it right at the window. It genuinely wants as much direct sun as possible — 4–6 hours of direct light daily is ideal.
  • 5Once settled, rotate the pot 90° every 2–4 weeks so all sides grow evenly toward the light.
